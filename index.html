Overview

Executive Summary

In our project, I address Horizon State University's growing challenge of declining retention during the first two years of undergraduate study. I identified that advisors currently lack a unified and proactive way to monitor academic, financial, and engagement risk factors, because this information is spread across disconnected systems. This fragmentation leads to delays, missed warnings, and inconsistent interventions, ultimately increasing the chances of academic failure or withdrawal. To solve this problem, I developed the Student Risk Monitoring Dashboard, a centralized platform built using artificially generated student data. My system brings together academic records, attendance, financial aid indicators, and engagement behaviors into one dashboard. By applying predictive analytics, the platform identifies early signs of students at risk of program dropout and provides advisors with real-time alerts and personalized recommendations that they can send to students. This allows advisors to intervene earlier and focus their attention on the students who need support the most.

Technical Design

Architecture & Data Design

System Architecture

The Student Risk Monitoring Dashboard operates across three integrated layers: Business Architecture, IS Architecture, and Tech Architecture, creating a unified, secure, and data-driven ecosystem that supports early detection and targeted student success interventions.

Enterprise Architecture

Business Architecture

Outlines core student and faculty processes including user authentication, profile access, at-risk student identification, and academic recommendation workflows. Captures engagement and monitoring activities such as attendance tracking, LMS logins, and analytics that inform early alerts and personalized student interventions.

Information Systems Architecture

Shows how student data flows through the system: synthetic CSV files are created and loaded, cached for performance, grouped by risk factors, and processed through predictive models. Includes the Streamlit dashboard for real-time monitoring, visual analytics, downloadable CSV exports, and automated email recommendations for at-risk students.

Technology Architecture

Built on Python runtime with Streamlit as the UI framework, local file storage for data persistence, and integrated security features including salted SHA-256 hashing, credential store, and TLS encryption. Utilizes Plotly for interactive visualizations and Pandas/NumPy for data manipulation.

Entity-Relationship Diagram

Database Schema

The data model integrates comprehensive student information across multiple dimensions:

  • Student Demographics: StudentID, first_generation_flag, gender, ethnicity, residence, age_at_entry
  • Academic Performance: Cumulative GPA, term GPA, academic_probation_flag, course_drop_count, credits_attempted, enrollment_status
  • Engagement Metrics: Attendance rate, LMS logins, discussion posts, tutoring sessions, advisor meetings, library visits, assignments_on_time_pct
  • Financial Indicators: Financial_aid_flag, outstanding_balance, work_hours_per_week
  • Risk Predictions: Dropout_probability, at_risk_flag (generated by predictive model)

Reflection

Lessons Learned & Team Reflection

Technical Learnings

  • Synthetic Data Realism: Generating a synthetic dataset that was realistic enough to validate models yet flexible for experimentation required repeated refinement and cross-checks against national benchmarks.
  • Model Optimization: Experimented with several feature combinations and risk scoring approaches to optimize both accuracy (92%) and interpretability for advisor use.
  • Dashboard Responsiveness: Ensuring the Streamlit dashboard remained responsive and interactive for large datasets tested data handling and caching strategies.
  • Architecture Importance: Having a clear solution architecture with business, IS, and technology layers helped develop the application without gaps and maintained consistency.

Key Decisions

  • Dataset Design: Balanced realism with model robustness by ensuring variables like GPA, attendance, and engagement mirrored real student behaviors while providing meaningful variance.
  • Visualization Strategy: Iteratively revised charts, color coding, and layouts based on usability feedback to help advisors quickly identify at-risk students without cognitive overload.
  • Feature Engineering: Prioritized academic, engagement, and financial indicators over demographic attributes to reduce bias and ensure fair predictions.
  • Batch Processing: Separated model training from dashboard usage to ensure efficiency and keep advisor focus on interventions rather than technical processes.

Team Collaboration

  • Distributed Responsibilities: One member led data generation, another predictive modeling, a third dashboard UI, a fourth system architecture, and a fifth deployment and documentation.
  • Collective Engagement: Despite lead roles, all members actively contributed to discussions, design decisions, testing, and improvements.
  • Cross-Validation: Working collaboratively allowed us to leverage each member's expertise, make informed decisions, and address challenges efficiently.
  • Aligned Outcomes: This approach ensured that architecture, dashboard, predictive models, and deployment were aligned with project objectives for early risk identification.

Challenges Overcome

  • Data Generation Complexity: Creating realistic synthetic data that reflected national patterns required extensive research and iterative testing.
  • Performance Optimization: Large datasets caused initial slow load times. Implemented caching and lazy loading to improve experience.
  • Visualization Clarity: Early chart designs were too complex. Iterative refinement significantly improved clarity, speed, and advisor usability.
  • Integration Testing: Ensuring predictions merged accurately with original dataset required robust integrity checks to prevent missing or mismatched records.
